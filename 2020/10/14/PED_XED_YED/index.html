Types of Elasticity of Demand

PED & XED & YED & PES

revision notes

Price elasticity of demand

Definition

  • Elasticity
    • A measure of how much one economic variable responds to changes in another economic variable
  • Price elasticity of demand
    • The responsiveness of the quantity demanded to a change in price, measured by dividing the percentage change in the quantity demanded of a product by the precentage change in the product's price \[PED = \frac{\% \Delta{QD}}{\% \Delta{P}}\]

Notice

  • ! PED \(\neq\) the gradient of the demand curve
  • ! take absolute value since the inverse relationship between demand and curve determines all PED value are negative
  • ! Economists are concerned with whether the value is greater or less than one
    • i.e. elastic or inelastic

PED classfication

PED classfications Economic indications Examples
PED\(>1\) elastic beverage(coco-cola, etc)
PED\(<1\) inelastic bread
PED\(=1\) unit elastic
PED\(\rightarrow \infty\) perfectly elastic equal to inifinity no real-life example
PED\(=0\) perfectly inelastic insulin

  • Elastic Demand
    • The percentage change in the quantity demanded is greater than the percentage change in price
    • Examples:
      • bevearge | coca-cola, pepsi, etc.
      • bagel | 甜甜圈
  • Inelastic Demand
    • The percentage change in the quantity demanded is less than the percentage change in price
    • Examples:
      • wheat
      • eggs
  • Unit-elastic Demand
    • The percentage change in the quantity demanded is equal to the percentage change in price
  • Perfectly elastic Demand
    • The quantity demanded is infinitely repsonsive to price
  • Perfectly inelastic Demand
    • The quantity demanded is completely unresponsive to price
    • Examples
      • insulin \(\rightarrow\) 胰岛素
        • some diabetics must take a certain amount of insulin each day
        • thus, an increase/decrease in price will NOT affect the required dose

Determinants of the Price Elasticity of Demand

  • The availability of close substitutes to the good
    • consumer react to a change in the price of aproduct depends on what alternatives they have to that product
      • a key constraint on a firm's pricing policies \(\implies\) the number of existed close substitutes
    • Examples:
      • gasoline
        • few alternatives
        • when price \(\uparrow \implies\) quantity demanded falls only a litte
      • pizza
        • many alternatives
        • when price \(\uparrow \implies\) quantity demanded is likely to fall substantially
  • Passage of Time
    • it takes consumers some time to adjust their buying habits
      • more time that passes, more elastic the demand for product becomes
  • Luxuries versus Necessities
    • luxuries usually have more elastic demand curve
    • Examples:
      • demand for bread is inelastic because the quantity that people buy is not very dependent on its price (necessity)
  • Definition of the Market
    • In a narrowly defined market, consumers have more substitutes available
      • more elastic demand will be
  • Share of a Good in a Consumer's Budget
    • goods that take only a small fraction of a consumer's budget tend to have less elastic demand
    • Examples:
      • for "big-ticket items"
        • houses, cars, and furniture take up a larger share in the average consumer's budget increase in the prices of these goods are likely to result in significant declines in the quantity demanded

The ralationship between Price Elasticity of Demand and Total Revenue

  • Total revenue
    • the total amount funds it receives from selling a good or service \[ TR = \text{price per unit x number of units } \]
  • Inelastic Demand
    • price and total revenue move in the same direction \[p \uparrow \implies TR \uparrow\]
  • Elastic Demand
    • price and total revenue move inversely \[p \uparrow \implies TR \downarrow\]
  • Demand Curve & Total Revenue & PED

Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand

Definition

  • Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand
    • a measure how the quantity demanded of one good responds to a change in the price of another good
    • calculated by the percentage change in the quantity demanded of one good divided by the percentage change in the price another good \[ XED = \frac{\% \Delta{QD_{a}}}{\% P_{b}}\]

XED Classification

  • XED is either positive or negative
  • substitutes | positive
    • goods that are typically used for the same purpose
    • the price of good A moves in the same direction with the quantity demanded of good B
      • examples
        • an increase in hot dog prices induces people to buy hamburgers instead
    • allow firm managers to measure whether products sold by other firms are close substitutes for their products
      • Examples:
        • Peipsi & Coca-cola spend heavily on advertising with the hope of convincing consumers that each cola tastes better than its rival
          • XED is used to examine the effectiveness of the advertisin campaign
          • if Coca-cola has a successful advertising campaign, when it increases the price of Coke, the percentage increase in sales of Pepsi should be smaller, the value of XED shoud have declined
  • complements | negative
    • goods that are typically used together
    • the price of good B moves in the opposite direction with the quantity demanded of good B
      • examples
        • an increase in price of computers reduces the quantity of software demanded
  • ! If XED=0 \(\implies\) these two goods are unrelated

Income Elasticity of Demand

Definition

  • Income Elasticity of Demand
    • The income elasticity meausres the responsiveness of the quantity demanded to changes in income \[YED = \frac{\% \Delta{QD}}{\% \Delta{\text{income}}}\]

YED Classification

  • normal goods | positive
    • higher incomes \(\implies\) higher quantity demanded
    • luxuries
      • The quantity demanded is very responsive to changes in income
        • consumers feel that they can do without these goods altogether if their incomes are too low
      • an \(10 \%\) change in income results in more than \(10\%\) increase in the quantity demanded
        • expensive jewelry and vacation homes are examples of luxuries
    • necessities
      • The quantity demanded is not very responsive to changes in income
        • consumers choose to buy some of these goods even when their incomes are low
      • an \(10 \%\) change in income results in more than \(10\%\) increase in the quantity demanded
        • food and clothing are examples of necessities
  • inferior goods | negative
    • higher incomes \(\implies\) lower quantity demanded
      • examples: ground beef with a high fat content

Notice

  • ! Most goods are normal goods
  • During periods of economic expansion when consumer income is rising, most firms can expect
    • the quantity demanded of their products will increase, ceteris paribus
    • sellers of luxuries can expect particular large increases
  • During recessions, falling consumer income can cause firms to experience increase in demand for inferior goods
    • Examples:
      • the quantity demand for bus trip increases as consumers cut back on air travel
      • supermarkets find that the quantity demanded for canned tuna increases relative to the demand for fresh salmon

Usefullness & Limitations

  • CASESTUDY1.1 Discuss how an understanding of elasticity concepts might be useful to the CEO of an airline company in making price and output decisions
    • The aim for a private firm such as an airline company is profit maximisation
      • increase total revenue
      • minimise cost of production
    • Demand for the airline's flight tickets are price-elastic
      • reasons for this
        • there are many substitutes available
        • air tickets take up a large proportion of an individual's income
      • price & non-price competition
        • price competition
          • lower its demand
            • price elastic \(\implies\) \(price \downarrow \rightarrow TR \uparrow\)
          • ! price reduction may NOT be feasible in the long run
            • may result in price wars
            • arline company may face retaliation from rival firms
        • non-price competition
          • product differentiation
            • to reduce the substitutability of its product
            • examples:
              • branding itself as a luxury brand and upgrading the comfort of passengers
            • the cost may increase in the short run, but the demand for the airline company will become more price-inelastic in the long run
              • allow the company to charge higher prices for its air tickets, increasing TR and increasing profits
    • Demand for the airline's flight tickets are income-elastic
      • reasons
        • tickets are expensive and is considered by many to be a luxury good
      • if the airline aims to maxmise profits, it can employ strategies to help the middle and lower class afford its air tickets as well
        • increases its customer base
        • increases market share of the firm as it caters to a wide variety of customers
      • example strategy
        • expanding its service to include a budget aire line which offers no-frills service
          • minimises cost of production \(\implies\) reduce price \(\implies\) become more affodable for the middle and lower income classes
          • thus it will result in an increase in the number of sources of revenue for the firm
        • however, the change in income has to be sustainable and long-term for such a measure to be most effective
          • if the change in income is a one-off phenomenon, high costs may be incurred and lead to a smaller profit margin
    • With the concept of XED, the airline company can identify its rivals and those it can work with to increase profits in the long run
      • substitutes&complements
        • a decrease in the price of rival airline companies will cause demand for the airline company to decrease
        • due to the massive number of airline companies, there is high chance of engaging in price and non-price competition
        • the airline company has to cooperate with firms that produce the complement goods or the airline company
          • emaples:
            • tour agencies and offer packages tha are value for money and are able to attract customers
              • helps increase the demand
    • Overall
      • PED is most relevant and useful as the airline company has direct influence over the prices of air tickets
      • XED and YED requires a change in income level or change in the prices of related goods first
        • The expected change has to be accurate in order for the measures to be effective
